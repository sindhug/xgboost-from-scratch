01 · The Data

A building, a thermostat, and a power bill

Ten readings: outdoor temperature versus the power a building consumed. Cold days and hot days both push power use up — heating and cooling both cost energy. That's the whole dataset we'll use for the entire video.

Temperature (°C) on the x-axis, power consumed on the y-axis.

02 · The Big Idea

Many small trees, added one at a time

A single small tree is a rough sketch. Boosting adds another small tree that focuses only on the leftover mistakes — the residuals — then another, and another, each nudging the prediction a little closer. This is different from Random Forest, which grows many trees independently and averages them.

Random Forest — parallel, then average
Boosting — sequential, each fixes the last
03 · Baseline

Start with the simplest possible guess: the mean

Before any tree, the model's first prediction for every single point is just the average power consumed:

mean = 8.8
(14+11+9+7+5+5+6+8+11+12) / 10
04 · Residuals

How wrong is that guess, for each point?

Subtract the mean from every actual value. That leftover error is the residual — and it's the new thing our first tree will try to predict.

TempPowerResidual
05 · Finding the Split

Which threshold best separates the residuals?

We test candidate thresholds. For each one, residuals get sent left or right, and we score the split by gain — how much better grouped the residuals are after the split than before. Watch the three candidates below:

temp ≤ 2.5   gain = 30.04 temp ≤ 7.5   gain = 34.23 temp ≤ 12.5   gain = 27.50
✓ 7.5 gives the highest gain — it becomes the root split.
06 · Growing the Tree

One split isn't enough — grow one more level

We could split either child further. Splitting the tiny left child only gains 4.5; splitting the bigger right child at 37.5 gains 35.04. We take the better one.

07 · Making a Prediction

Follow a new point down the tree

To predict for a new temperature, start at the baseline, walk down the tree, and add whatever correction the landing leaf holds. Try x = 33°C:

F(33) = 8.8 + (-2.13) = 6.67
08 · Learning Rate (η)

Turn down the volume on every correction

η (eta) scales each tree's correction before it's added. Smaller η means smaller, safer steps — and larger residuals left over for the next tree to learn from. Try it:

raw correction
+3.70
η × correction
+1.85

Prediction for x=4: 8.8 + 0.50 × 3.70 = 10.65

09 · Tree Capacity & Stopping

Gamma: a toll booth for every new leaf

A split only happens if its gain clears γ (gamma). Below that bar, the gain is treated as not worth a new leaf, and the node stays as-is. Slide gamma up and watch both candidate splits from step 6 react:

Left child split  (gain 4.5) SPLIT
Right child split  (gain 35.04) SPLIT
10 · Regularization

Lambda: shrink every leaf toward zero

λ (lambda) adds "virtual" points to every leaf, shrinking both the leaf's correction and its similarity score. Only strong, consistent groups of residuals survive a big λ:

leaf (temp ≤ 7.5)
+3.70
leaf (temp > 7.5)
-0.93

Split gain at this λ: 34.23  →  worth splitting

11 · What Makes It "Extreme"

Still gradient boosting — just tuned for scale

  • 🎯 Uses both the error and its curvature to pick splits faster
  • 🛡️ Bakes in regularization (λ and γ) to resist overfitting
  • 🧩 Handles missing and sparse data natively
  • ⚡ Heavily optimized — the same math, just fast enough for millions of rows
12 · Reality Check

Would the real XGBoost library agree?
